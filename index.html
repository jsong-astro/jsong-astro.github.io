About Me

Ph.D. Student in Astrophysics · USTC & University of Tokyo

I am a Ph.D. student in astrophysics at the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC), working under the supervision of Prof. Xu Kong. I am currently a visiting researcher at the University of Tokyo, collaborating with Prof. Masami Ouchi (May 2025 – May 2026).

My research focuses on the formation and evolution of galaxies, with a particular emphasis on understanding how galaxies assemble their stellar mass, grow in size, and transform their morphologies across cosmic time. I approach these questions by combining deep, multi-wavelength imaging from JWST and HST with advanced techniques including spatially resolved SED fitting, machine-learning-based morphological classification, and statistical analyses of galaxy environments. A central theme of my work is leveraging the unprecedented near-infrared capabilities of JWST to derive reliable physical properties — such as stellar mass, star formation rate, and their radial profiles — for large galaxy samples out to z ~ 4.

My recent work has provided new insights into the transition from outside-in to inside-out galaxy growth at z ~ 2, resolved a long-standing discrepancy between resolved and unresolved stellar mass estimates by demonstrating the critical role of rest-frame NIR coverage, and revealed how the local environment regulates the quenching of the most massive galaxies. I am also actively developing efficient galaxy morphological classification frameworks in preparation for next-generation wide-field surveys such as CSST and Euclid.
